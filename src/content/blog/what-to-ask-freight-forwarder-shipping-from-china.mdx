When small importers prepare their first shipment from China, the first question is often: "How much is shipping?"

That question is not enough. A freight forwarder quote can depend on product type, carton size, weight, trade term, pickup location, destination address, documents, customs process, duties, taxes, insurance, and final delivery method. Two quotes can look very different because they may cover different responsibilities.

This guide gives you a practical question list for talking with freight forwarders before shipping from China. It is for preparation only, not legal or customs advice.

Use it with the <a href="/china-sourcing/">China Sourcing Guide</a>, the <a href="/blog/exw-fob-cif-ddp-china-sourcing/">EXW, FOB, CIF, and DDP guide</a>, and the <a href="/tools/supplier-comparison-sheet/">Supplier Comparison Sheet</a> when comparing quotes.

## Who this guide is for

This guide is for buyers who are preparing a first shipment from China or comparing supplier shipping quotes with freight forwarder quotes.

It is especially useful for:

- Small importers
- Amazon sellers
- Shopify sellers
- First-time sourcing buyers
- Canton Fair buyers
- Buyers preparing first shipments from China
- Buyers comparing supplier shipping quotes and freight forwarder quotes

If you are still confirming the supplier order itself, review the <a href="/blog/what-to-confirm-before-first-order-chinese-supplier/">first order confirmation checklist</a> before arranging shipment.

## Why freight forwarder questions matter

Freight forwarder questions affect more than the shipping price. They affect how clearly you understand the shipment.

Good questions help clarify:

- Total landed cost
- Shipping method
- Customs process
- Required documents
- Delivery timeline
- Warehouse or marketplace delivery
- Insurance options
- Risk during shipment
- Communication after goods leave the supplier

Shipping details can vary by product, destination, forwarder, supplier, and trade term. Confirm all quote details in writing and do not rely on the cheapest quote only.

## Important note before asking a freight forwarder

This guide is for practical preparation only. Shipping, customs, duties, taxes, documentation, insurance, delivery, and import requirements can vary by product and destination market.

Use a freight forwarder, customs broker, or professional support when needed. Do not use this guide as legal or customs advice.

## Step 1: Tell the forwarder what you are shipping

A freight forwarder cannot give a useful quote without basic shipment details.

Prepare:

- Product name
- Product category
- Quantity
- Number of cartons
- Carton weight
- Carton dimensions
- Product value
- Supplier pickup location
- Destination country
- Delivery address type
- Whether goods go to your address, warehouse, or marketplace warehouse

If your product is fragile, high-value, battery-powered, liquid, magnetic, oversized, branded, or regulated in your destination market, say so early and use professional support where needed.

## Step 2: Confirm the trade term

Trade terms affect who handles pickup, export documents, freight, destination charges, and delivery responsibility.

Ask the forwarder:

- Is this quote based on EXW, FOB, CIF, DDP, or another term?
- What named place or port is assumed?
- Who handles pickup from the supplier?
- Who handles export documents?
- What part can the forwarder manage?
- What part is still the supplier's responsibility?
- What part remains the buyer's responsibility?

If EXW, FOB, CIF, or DDP is unclear, read the <a href="/blog/exw-fob-cif-ddp-china-sourcing/">trade terms guide</a> before comparing quotes.

## Step 3: Ask what shipping method is being quoted

The shipping method affects timeline, cost, risk, and delivery process.

Common options may include:

- Air freight
- Sea freight
- Express courier
- Rail or truck in some routes where relevant

Ask:

- What shipping method is this quote based on?
- What timeline is realistic?
- What events can delay this method?
- Does the quote cover pickup and final delivery?
- Is this method suitable for the product and destination?

Do not rely on a guaranteed timeline unless it is clearly confirmed in writing. Timelines can vary by route, season, documents, customs process, carrier availability, and final delivery address.

## Step 4: Ask what is included in the quote

A quote amount is only useful when the included scope is clear.

Ask whether the quote includes:

- Pickup
- Origin charges
- Export handling
- Freight
- Insurance if included
- Customs support if included
- Destination charges
- Duties and taxes if included
- Last-mile delivery
- Warehouse appointment if relevant

Ask the forwarder to write the included items clearly. This makes it easier to compare quotes from different providers.

## Step 5: Ask what is not included

Excluded costs are often more important than the headline quote.

Ask whether the quote excludes:

- Duties and taxes
- Storage
- Inspection
- Customs exam fees
- Remote delivery
- Address correction
- Extra handling
- Delay charges
- Documentation changes
- Warehouse appointment changes

Do not treat a cheaper quote as better until you understand what it excludes.

## Step 6: Ask about customs and import responsibility

Customs requirements vary by destination and product. This is where small buyers should be careful.

Ask:

- Who prepares the shipment documents?
- Who acts as importer if that applies?
- Is a customs broker needed?
- What product information is required?
- Does the forwarder provide customs support?
- Is product classification or HS code support needed from a professional?
- What happens if customs requests more information?

Do not guess HS codes, duties, taxes, or import rules from a general article. Use qualified customs or compliance support when needed.

## Step 7: Ask what documents are needed

Documents should be discussed before goods leave the supplier.

Ask whether the shipment needs:

- Commercial invoice
- Packing list
- Bill of lading or air waybill if relevant
- Certificate or test documents if needed
- Product description
- Supplier contact
- Buyer information
- Delivery address
- Marketplace or warehouse delivery details if relevant

Document requirements can vary by product, destination, buyer setup, and shipping method. Confirm details in writing before payment.

## Step 8: Ask about duties, taxes, and landed cost

A freight quote is not always the total landed cost. Some quotes include more costs than others.

Ask:

- Are duties and taxes included?
- Who pays duties and taxes?
- How are they estimated?
- What is excluded from the estimate?
- Are destination charges included?
- What costs could appear after arrival?

Do not compare freight quotes without understanding landed cost. A quote with a low shipping amount may still leave important costs outside the quote.

## Step 9: Ask about delivery address and final mile

Final delivery can change the quote and timeline.

Confirm whether the delivery address is:

- Residential address
- Business address
- Warehouse
- Marketplace warehouse
- Appointment delivery location

Ask:

- Is final delivery included?
- Is a delivery appointment needed?
- Is liftgate, unloading, or special handling needed?
- What delivery phone number should be used?
- What happens if the address is incomplete or incorrect?
- Are there remote delivery or address correction charges?

Address accuracy matters. Save the full delivery address, contact person, phone number, and delivery notes in writing.

## Step 10: Ask about cargo insurance and risk handling

Insurance details vary by shipment and provider. Do not assume insurance is included unless the quote says so.

Ask:

- Is insurance included?
- What is covered?
- What is excluded?
- How are claims handled?
- What proof is needed for a claim?
- Are high-value, fragile, or damage-sensitive goods handled differently?
- What happens if goods are delayed, lost, or damaged?

For higher-value or higher-risk shipments, use professional support and confirm the insurance details before payment.

## Step 11: Ask how shipment updates are handled

Good communication matters after goods leave the supplier.

Ask:

- How will tracking be shared?
- What milestone updates are provided?
- Who is the contact person?
- How are delays communicated?
- How are document issues communicated?
- How are customs issues communicated?
- How is delivery confirmation provided?

If the forwarder cannot explain the update process before payment, shipment communication may be difficult later.

## Step 12: Compare forwarder quotes fairly

Do not compare freight forwarders by quote amount only. Compare the scope.

Use the <a href="/tools/supplier-comparison-sheet/">Supplier Comparison Sheet</a> or your own spreadsheet to compare:

- Shipping method
- Trade term
- Included costs
- Excluded costs
- Timeline
- Customs support
- Duties and taxes handling
- Insurance
- Communication quality
- Risk notes

If two quotes use different methods or include different responsibilities, they are not directly comparable.

- Product value prepared
- Supplier pickup location confirmed
- Destination country confirmed
- Delivery address type confirmed
- Trade term confirmed
- Shipping method requested
- Included costs requested
- Excluded costs requested

## Freight forwarder message template

Copy and adapt this message:

```text
Hello [Forwarder Name],

We are preparing a shipment from China and would like to compare shipping options. Please confirm:

1. Shipping method
2. Trade term assumed in the quote
3. Pickup location and delivery address covered
4. What costs are included
5. What costs are excluded
6. Estimated timeline
7. Required documents
8. Customs handling and importer responsibility
9. Whether duties and taxes are included
10. Insurance options
11. Tracking and update process
12. What happens if there is a delay or customs issue

Please also confirm what shipment details you need from our supplier before giving a final quote.

Thank you.
```

## Red flags when choosing a freight forwarder

Be careful when:

- Quote does not say what is included
- Trade term is unclear
- Timeline sounds unrealistic
- Customs responsibility is vague
- Duties and taxes are not explained
- Documents are not confirmed
- Communication is slow or inconsistent
- Forwarder pushes payment before details are clear
- Quote is much cheaper but exclusions are unclear

These are reasons to slow down, ask clearer questions, and confirm details in writing.

## Common mistakes small importers make

### Comparing freight quotes without checking trade terms

An EXW quote and a DDP-style quote can cover very different responsibilities.

### Choosing only the cheapest quote

The cheapest quote may exclude duties, taxes, destination charges, documents, insurance, or final delivery.

### Ignoring duties and taxes

Duties and taxes may be included, excluded, estimated, or handled separately depending on the quote and destination. Confirm before payment.

### Not confirming delivery address type

Residential, business, warehouse, and marketplace warehouse deliveries may need different handling.

### Not asking what documents are needed

Document problems can slow down shipment. Ask early.

### Not saving written quote details

Save the quote, included costs, exclusions, timeline, documents, and contact person.

### Assuming supplier shipping quote and forwarder quote cover the same scope

Supplier quotes and forwarder quotes can use different assumptions. Compare what each quote includes.

### Not planning for customs or delays

Customs, documents, inspections, delays, and delivery appointments can affect the shipment. Build some buffer into the plan.

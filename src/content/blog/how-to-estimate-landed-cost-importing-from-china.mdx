Small importers often compare Chinese supplier quotes by looking at the unit price first. That is understandable, but it is also where many budget mistakes start.

The real cost of importing from China may include more than the product price. Packaging, customization, sample costs, freight, insurance, destination charges, duties, taxes, customs or broker support, final delivery, inspection, and unexpected handling can all affect the total amount you need to plan for.

This guide helps you build a practical landed cost estimate before you accept a quote or arrange payment. It is a preparation guide, not legal, tax, or customs advice.

Use it with the <a href="/china-sourcing/">China Sourcing Guide</a>, the <a href="/blog/exw-fob-cif-ddp-china-sourcing/">EXW, FOB, CIF, and DDP guide</a>, the <a href="/blog/what-to-ask-freight-forwarder-shipping-from-china/">freight forwarder question guide</a>, and the <a href="/tools/supplier-comparison-sheet/">Supplier Comparison Sheet</a>.

## Who this guide is for

This guide is for buyers who already have supplier quotes, freight quotes, or early order details and want to understand the bigger cost picture before payment.

It is especially useful for:

- Small importers
- Amazon sellers
- Shopify sellers
- First-time sourcing buyers
- Canton Fair buyers
- Overseas buyers comparing supplier quotes
- Buyers preparing their first shipment from China

If you are still negotiating MOQ, unit price, or quote scope, review the <a href="/blog/how-to-negotiate-moq-price-chinese-suppliers/">MOQ and price negotiation guide</a> before treating any quote as final.

## What landed cost means in plain English

Landed cost is the estimated total cost to get goods from the supplier to the final destination where you can use, sell, inspect, or store them.

It is not only the unit price. It is also not always the same as the shipping cost.

Depending on your product, destination, trade term, shipping method, supplier, and freight forwarder, landed cost may include:

- Product cost
- Packaging or customization
- Sample cost if relevant
- Freight
- Insurance if used
- Duties and taxes if applicable
- Customs or broker support
- Destination charges
- Final delivery
- Inspection or quality control
- Extra handling, storage, or delay costs

Landed cost can vary by product, destination, trade term, shipping method, supplier, and freight forwarder. Confirm all cost assumptions in writing before payment.

## Why unit price can be misleading

A low supplier unit price may look attractive, but it may exclude important costs.

Check whether the quote includes or excludes:

- Unit packaging
- Inner boxes or cartons
- Custom labels or barcodes
- Domestic pickup
- Export handling
- International freight
- Cargo insurance
- Customs support
- Duties and taxes
- Destination charges
- Final delivery
- Inspection or testing
- Storage or delay costs

Do not rely on unit price alone. A supplier with a slightly higher unit price may still be better if packaging, lead time, communication, quality control, and shipping scope are clearer.

## Step 1: Start with supplier product cost

Begin with the numbers from the supplier quote.

Record:

- Unit price
- MOQ
- Quantity
- Price breaks
- Currency
- Price validity
- Packaging included or excluded
- Customization included or excluded
- Sample fee deduction if agreed

If the product specification is unclear, the cost estimate will be weak. Confirm material, size, color, packaging, logo, and other product details before comparing suppliers.

For supplier quote structure, use the <a href="/blog/how-to-compare-chinese-suppliers-before-sample-order/">supplier comparison guide</a> and the <a href="/blog/what-to-confirm-before-first-order-chinese-supplier/">first order confirmation checklist</a>.

## Step 2: Confirm packaging and preparation costs

Packaging can change the real cost per unit. It can also affect carton size, freight weight, damage risk, and sales channel requirements.

Ask whether the quote includes:

- Unit packaging
- Inner box
- Outer carton
- Labels
- Barcode or SKU if needed
- Logo or custom packaging
- Carton marks
- Palletizing if relevant

If packaging is not included, ask for the extra cost and confirm whether it affects MOQ, lead time, or carton details.

## Step 3: Confirm trade term and quote scope

The same product price can mean different things depending on EXW, FOB, CIF, DDP, or another trade term.

Before comparing quotes, confirm:

- Trade term
- Named place or port
- Pickup responsibility
- Export handling
- Freight responsibility
- Destination responsibility
- Final delivery address if relevant
- Included costs
- Excluded costs

If trade terms are unclear, read the <a href="/blog/exw-fob-cif-ddp-china-sourcing/">EXW, FOB, CIF, and DDP guide</a> before comparing total cost.

## Step 4: Add freight and shipping costs

Freight can be a major part of landed cost, but freight quotes are not always built on the same scope.

Confirm:

- Shipping method
- Supplier pickup location
- International freight
- Destination delivery
- Warehouse or final address
- Whether duties or taxes are included
- What costs are excluded
- Quote validity

Use the <a href="/blog/what-to-ask-freight-forwarder-shipping-from-china/">freight forwarder question guide</a> before accepting a shipping quote. Ask what is included, what is excluded, and what could change after pickup or arrival.

## Step 5: Add duties, taxes, and customs-related costs

Duties, taxes, and customs requirements vary by product and destination market. Do not guess them from a general article.

Your estimate may need to consider:

- Import duty if applicable
- Tax, VAT, or GST if applicable
- Customs broker or clearance support
- Product classification support when needed
- Documents required for your product and destination

This is a practical preparation guide, not legal, tax, or customs advice. Use a freight forwarder, customs broker, accountant, or professional support when needed.

## Step 6: Add insurance and risk-related costs

Insurance details vary by shipment and provider. Do not assume insurance is included unless the quote says so.

Ask:

- Is cargo insurance included?
- What goods are covered?
- What is excluded?
- What proof is needed for a claim?
- Are fragile or high-value goods handled differently?
- What happens if goods are delayed, lost, or damaged?

Confirm coverage in writing before payment. For higher-value or higher-risk shipments, use professional support.

## Step 7: Add inspection or quality control costs

Quality checks can be part of your total order cost. They may not appear in the supplier unit price.

Depending on the product and risk level, consider:

- Pre-shipment inspection
- Product testing if needed
- Sample comparison
- Packaging checks
- Rework cost if defects are found
- Inspection timing before final payment

For a more detailed process, use the <a href="/blog/chinese-supplier-quality-control-checklist-before-bulk-order/">Chinese supplier quality control checklist</a>.

## Step 8: Add destination and final delivery costs

Costs after arrival can surprise first-time importers. They vary by destination, shipment method, delivery address, and service scope.

Ask about:

- Destination port or terminal charges
- Warehouse delivery
- Residential or business address delivery
- Appointment delivery if needed
- Remote area delivery
- Storage or delay charges
- Address correction fees
- Extra handling

Do not add random numbers. Ask your freight forwarder, customs broker, warehouse, or relevant service provider what may apply to your shipment.

## Step 9: Add a buffer for unexpected costs

For a first import, avoid building a budget that works only if everything goes perfectly.

Possible extra costs can come from:

- Delays
- Document corrections
- Extra handling
- Storage
- Customs questions
- Repackaging
- Rerouting
- Supplier changes
- Delivery appointment issues

The buffer is not a precise calculation. It is a planning habit that helps you avoid deciding based on a budget that is too tight.

## Step 10: Compare landed cost by supplier, not just unit price

Once you have the main cost fields, compare suppliers by estimated landed cost and risk, not just by unit price.

Use the <a href="/tools/supplier-comparison-sheet/">Supplier Comparison Sheet</a> to compare:

- Supplier product cost
- Packaging and customization
- Trade term
- Freight scope
- Duties and taxes handling
- Inspection needs
- Destination delivery
- Communication quality
- Risk notes

A supplier with a slightly higher unit price may be the better choice if the quote is clearer, packaging is included, lead time is realistic, and the supplier confirms details in writing.

## Landed cost formula

Use this as a planning formula:

```text
Estimated landed cost =
supplier product cost
+ packaging / customization
+ freight
+ insurance
+ duties and taxes
+ customs or broker support
+ destination charges
+ final delivery
+ inspection or QC costs
+ risk buffer
```

This is a planning formula, not a tax or customs calculation. The exact cost can vary by product, destination, trade term, shipping method, supplier, forwarder, and timing.

## Questions to ask before accepting a landed cost estimate

Ask these questions before you treat the estimate as ready:

- What trade term is this quote based on?
- What costs are included?
- What costs are excluded?
- Does the freight quote include duties or taxes?
- Who handles customs?
- What documents are needed?
- What final delivery address is covered?
- Are there possible destination charges?
- What happens if shipment is delayed?
- How long is the quote valid?
- What details must be confirmed before payment?

Save the answers in writing. If the answer affects payment, shipping, customs, delivery, or product compliance, do not rely on verbal promises only.

## Common mistakes small importers make

### Comparing only supplier unit price

Unit price is only one part of the decision. Packaging, freight, duties, taxes, inspection, and final delivery can change the total cost.

### Ignoring trade terms

EXW, FOB, CIF, and DDP quotes may cover different responsibilities. Compare scope before comparing price.

### Assuming shipping quote includes duties and taxes

Some quotes include them, some exclude them, and some estimate them separately. Confirm before payment.

### Not checking destination charges

Arrival-side costs can matter. Ask what may apply before the goods ship.

### Forgetting packaging costs

Packaging affects cost, carton size, damage risk, labeling, and sales channel readiness.

### Ignoring inspection or testing costs

Quality control is part of order planning, especially for custom products, new suppliers, or higher-risk goods.

### Not using professional support when needed

Use a freight forwarder, customs broker, accountant, inspection company, or compliance specialist when the shipment, product, or destination creates uncertainty.

### Not saving written cost details

Keep supplier quotes, freight quotes, included costs, exclusions, trade terms, delivery scope, and quote validity in one place.

### Not adding a risk buffer

A budget with no room for delays, documents, storage, or handling issues can make a small order more stressful than expected.

## Red flags when estimating landed cost

Slow down when:

- Quote does not state the trade term
- Freight quote does not say what is included
- Supplier avoids packaging details
- Duties and taxes are unclear
- Destination delivery is vague
- Documents are not confirmed
- Total cost depends on verbal promises
- Quote is much cheaper but exclusions are unclear
- The final delivery address is not confirmed
- Quote validity is missing

These signs do not always mean the supplier or forwarder is wrong. They mean the cost estimate is not ready yet.

A sample that looks good is not the end of quality control. Before placing a bulk order with a Chinese supplier, you still need to confirm what will be produced, how it will be packed, how defects will be handled, and what the supplier agrees to in writing.

Bulk order problems often start before production. The buyer approves a sample, but the final specification is vague. Packaging is not confirmed. Defect expectations are never discussed. Inspection is left until the last minute. By the time goods are finished, every correction is harder.

Use this checklist after you have compared suppliers and reviewed samples. If you are earlier in the process, start with the <a href="/blog/how-to-compare-chinese-suppliers-before-sample-order/">supplier comparison guide</a> and the <a href="/blog/how-to-request-samples-from-chinese-suppliers/">sample request guide</a> first.

## What to confirm before a bulk order

Before placing a bulk order with a Chinese supplier, confirm:

- Final product specifications
- Approved sample details
- Packaging and labeling
- Acceptable and unacceptable defects
- Supplier quality control process
- Pre-shipment photos, videos, or inspection plan
- Certification or testing needs where relevant
- Defect handling before final payment
- Written order confirmation

Do not rely on verbal promises or lowest price only. Quality requirements can vary by product and market, so important details should be confirmed in writing.

## Why quality control matters before a bulk order

Quality control is not only something that happens after production. Many problems can be reduced before production starts if the buyer and supplier agree on the same details.

Common causes of quality problems include:

- Unclear specifications
- Sample and mass production differences
- Packaging changes
- Material substitutions
- Rushed production
- Unclear defect standards
- No inspection plan
- Unclear responsibility after shipment

No checklist can guarantee a problem-free order. The point is to make expectations clearer before money, materials, and production time are committed.

## Step 1: Confirm the approved sample

The approved sample should be a reference, not a vague memory.

Record:

- Which sample was approved
- Sample date
- Sample photos
- Sample version
- Material, size, and color
- Packaging
- Logo or customization details
- Differences allowed or not allowed

Sample approval does not guarantee bulk production quality. It gives you a reference point for the supplier, inspector, and your own review.

## Step 2: Confirm product specifications in writing

Before production, turn the sample decision into written specifications.

Confirm:

- Material
- Dimensions
- Color
- Function
- Components
- Packaging
- Logo
- Label
- Accessories
- Manual
- Carton information if relevant

If the supplier changes any material, component, or packaging detail, ask them to explain whether it affects price, lead time, quality, or compliance.

## Step 3: Confirm packaging and labeling

Packaging is part of the product experience and can affect cost, damage risk, storage, and sales channel requirements.

Confirm:

- Unit packaging
- Inner box
- Outer carton
- Label position
- Barcode or SKU if needed
- Warning labels if applicable
- Packaging photos
- Whether packaging is included in the unit price

If your destination market or sales channel has specific packaging or labeling requirements, confirm those requirements separately and use professional support when needed.

## Step 4: Define acceptable and unacceptable defects

Do not wait until inspection day to decide what counts as a problem.

Discuss:

- Appearance defects
- Functional defects
- Color differences
- Size tolerance
- Broken packaging
- Missing accessories
- Wrong label
- Wrong quantity
- Safety or compliance concerns

Inspection standards should be confirmed in writing and can vary by product, market, and buyer risk level. If you use AQL or another inspection framework, confirm the details with a professional inspector or quality specialist instead of relying on assumptions.

## Step 5: Ask about the supplier's quality control process

Ask the supplier how they check goods before shipment.

Useful questions:

- Are incoming materials checked?
- Are there in-process checks during production?
- Is there a final inspection before shipment?
- Can the supplier compare production goods against the approved sample?
- Can the supplier share production photos?
- Who checks the goods before shipment?
- What happens if defects are found?

The supplier's answer will not prove the order is safe, but it helps you understand whether quality is being discussed seriously.

## Step 6: Decide whether to use third-party inspection

For higher-risk products, larger orders, custom goods, or regulated categories, buyers may need third-party inspection or professional compliance support.

Inspection may be useful when:

- You are working with a new supplier
- The product is customized
- The order value is meaningful for your business
- Packaging or labeling is important
- Defects would be expensive to fix after shipment
- The product has safety or compliance concerns

Inspection cannot prove everything. It usually checks a defined sample of goods against defined requirements. Confirm inspection standards before production so the supplier knows what will be checked.

## Step 7: Confirm certification and compliance needs

Certification requirements vary by product and destination market. Do not assume that a certificate shown during sales discussion applies to your exact product or market.

Ask:

- What documents are available?
- Do certificates or test reports match the exact product?
- Do documents show the correct product model or material?
- Are report dates current enough for your intended use?
- Does your destination market require extra testing?
- Should you use professional compliance support?

If compliance matters, confirm requirements before production and before shipment.

## Step 8: Confirm pre-shipment photos or videos

Photos or videos can help you catch obvious issues, but they are not a full inspection replacement.

Ask for:

- Product photos
- Packaging photos
- Carton photos
- Quantity overview
- Label photos
- Sample comparison photos
- Short function videos if relevant

Use photos as a practical check, not as a guarantee.

## Step 9: Confirm defect handling before payment

Discuss defect handling before final payment is due.

Confirm what happens if defects are found:

- Replacement
- Rework
- Discount
- Credit
- Responsibility for shipping or correction
- Timing for fixing the issue
- Written confirmation before final payment

Do not rely on verbal promises only. Defect handling should be specific enough that both sides understand what happens next.

## Step 10: Keep written confirmation

Keep all important order details in writing.

Save:

- Quotation
- Invoice
- Product specs
- Approved sample notes
- Packaging details
- Payment terms
- Lead time
- Inspection plan
- Defect handling agreement

This written record helps you, the supplier, your freight forwarder, and any inspector work from the same information.

## Quality control checklist before a bulk order

Copy this checklist before confirming the order:

- Approved sample identified
- Sample photos saved
- Final product specifications confirmed
- Material confirmed
- Dimensions confirmed
- Color confirmed
- Function confirmed
- Packaging confirmed
- Labeling confirmed
- Accessories confirmed
- Manual or insert confirmed if needed
- Acceptable defects discussed
- Unacceptable defects discussed
- Supplier QC process discussed
- Third-party inspection need reviewed
- Certification or testing needs reviewed
- Pre-shipment photo or video plan confirmed
- Defect handling confirmed
- Written order confirmation saved

## Supplier quality confirmation message template

Copy and adapt this message before confirming a bulk order:

```text
Hello [Supplier Name],

Before we confirm the bulk order, please confirm the following details in writing:

1. Final product specification
2. Approved sample reference
3. Material, size, color, and function
4. Packaging and labeling details
5. Production lead time
6. Quality control process
7. Acceptable and unacceptable defects
8. Certification or test documents if available
9. Pre-shipment photo or inspection process
10. Defect handling after inspection or delivery

Please confirm these details before we arrange the next payment step.

Thank you.
```

## Red flags before placing a bulk order

Be careful when:

- The supplier avoids written confirmation
- Sample details are unclear
- The quote does not include packaging details
- The supplier refuses to discuss inspection
- Certification documents do not match the product
- Lead time keeps changing
- The supplier pushes final payment before details are clear
- Answers are vague or inconsistent

These signs do not always mean the supplier is unusable. They mean you need clearer written confirmation before moving forward.

## Common mistakes small buyers make

### Assuming sample quality equals bulk quality

A good sample is useful, but production still needs clear specs and quality checks.

### Not confirming specs in writing

If specifications are only in chat fragments, mistakes are easier.

### Not checking packaging

Packaging affects cost, damage risk, labeling, and customer experience.

### Not discussing defects

If no one defines unacceptable defects, inspection discussions become harder later.

### Not planning inspection

Inspection requirements should be discussed before production, not only after goods are finished.

### Not checking certification relevance

Certification needs depend on the product and destination market.

### Choosing only by lowest price

The lowest price can hide unclear specs, weaker packaging, limited quality checks, or poor follow-up.

Your first order with a Chinese supplier should not be based only on price and a sample that looked acceptable. Before payment, you need to confirm what the supplier will produce, what is included in the quote, how the goods will be packed, when production starts, and what happens if there is a quality problem.

This is not about making sourcing risk disappear. It is about reducing confusion before money and production time are committed.

Use this checklist after you have compared suppliers, discussed samples, and negotiated MOQ or pricing. If you are earlier in the process, start with the <a href="/china-sourcing/">China Sourcing Guide</a> and the <a href="/tools/supplier-comparison-sheet/">Supplier Comparison Sheet</a>.

## What to confirm before payment

Before placing your first order with a Chinese supplier, confirm:

- Final product specification
- Approved sample reference
- MOQ and final order quantity
- Unit price and what is included
- Packaging and labeling
- Customization details
- Production lead time
- Payment terms
- Trade terms and shipping responsibility
- Quality control process
- Inspection plan if needed
- Defect handling
- Written confirmation

Do not rely on verbal promises or the lowest price only. Requirements can vary by product, supplier, and destination market.

This guide is for buyers preparing to place their first order with a Chinese supplier.

It is especially useful for:

- Small importers

## Why first orders need extra confirmation

First orders carry extra risk because the supplier relationship is still new and both sides may be working from different assumptions.

Common weak points include:

- Supplier relationship is new
- Sample and bulk production may differ
- Specifications may be unclear
- Payment terms may not be fully understood
- Packaging details may be missing
- Lead time may be vague
- Quality control process may not be confirmed

A first order is also where your internal process begins. If you record details carefully now, every later reorder is easier to manage.

## Step 1: Confirm the final product specification

Start with the product itself. A quote is only useful if everyone understands the same product.

Confirm:

- Product name and model
- Material
- Size
- Color
- Function
- Accessories
- Logo or customization
- Packaging
- Label
- Manual or insert if needed
- Acceptable differences

If a detail matters to your customer, platform, or destination market, it should not live only in a chat message.

## Step 2: Confirm the approved sample reference

The approved sample should become a reference for production, but sample approval does not guarantee bulk production quality.

Record:

- Which sample was approved
- Sample date
- Sample photos
- Sample version
- Changes after sample approval
- Whether the bulk order should match the sample

If you have not clearly requested and reviewed samples yet, use the <a href="/blog/how-to-request-samples-from-chinese-suppliers/">sample request guide</a> before moving into a first order.

## Step 3: Confirm MOQ and quantity

MOQ and final quantity affect price, packaging, production planning, and sometimes customization.

Confirm:

- Final order quantity
- MOQ
- Color or size breakdown
- Packaging quantity
- Carton quantity if relevant
- Whether price changes if quantity changes

If MOQ, trial order quantity, or price breaks are still unclear, review the <a href="/blog/how-to-negotiate-moq-price-chinese-suppliers/">MOQ and price negotiation guide</a> before accepting the quote.

## Step 4: Confirm unit price and what is included

Do not compare unit prices until you know what each price includes.

Confirm:

- Unit price
- Currency
- Price validity
- Packaging included or not
- Customization included or not
- Inspection cost if relevant
- Sample cost deduction if agreed
- What is not included

Price is only useful when the scope is clear.

If you need to turn the supplier quote into a fuller budget, use the <a href="/blog/how-to-estimate-landed-cost-importing-from-china/">landed cost guide</a> before comparing unit price, packaging, freight, duties, taxes, destination charges, and inspection costs.

## Step 5: Confirm payment terms before payment

Payment terms vary by supplier and order. Confirm the details in writing before payment.

Ask:

- What deposit is required?
- When is the balance due?
- What payment method is expected?
- When does production start?
- What documents are provided?
- What happens if production is delayed?

This is not legal advice. Use professional legal, compliance, or purchasing support when order risk is high.

## Step 6: Confirm trade terms and shipping responsibility

Trade terms affect responsibility, cost, and risk. Buyers should understand the practical meaning before payment and use professional support when needed.

If EXW, FOB, CIF, or DDP is unclear, review the <a href="/blog/exw-fob-cif-ddp-china-sourcing/">plain-English trade terms guide</a> before you accept the quote or arrange payment.

If shipping responsibility, documents, customs support, or delivery address details are still unclear, use the <a href="/blog/what-to-ask-freight-forwarder-shipping-from-china/">freight forwarder question guide</a> before you compare shipping quotes.

Discuss:

- Whether the quote is EXW, FOB, CIF, DDP, or another term
- Shipping port if relevant
- Who arranges freight
- Who handles documents
- Who pays which costs
- Whether the quote includes shipping

Keep this practical. The goal is to know who is responsible for which part of the shipment, not to turn a sourcing email into a legal document.

## Step 7: Confirm production lead time

Lead time is not just a number. Ask when it starts.

Confirm:

- Production lead time
- Whether lead time starts after sample approval
- Whether lead time starts after deposit
- Whether final artwork must be confirmed first
- Whether material availability affects timing
- Busy season or holiday risk
- How delays will be communicated

If timing matters for launch, inventory, or seasonal selling, write the lead time assumptions down.

## Step 8: Confirm packaging and labeling

Packaging details are easy to miss until goods are already produced.

Confirm:

- Unit packaging
- Inner box
- Outer carton
- Logo
- Barcode or SKU if needed
- Warning labels if applicable
- Carton mark
- Packaging photos before shipment
- Whether packaging is included in price

Packaging and labeling requirements can vary by product and destination market. Confirm your own requirements before production.

## Step 9: Confirm quality control and inspection

Quality control should be discussed before production starts.

Confirm:

- Supplier internal QC process
- Pre-shipment photos
- Final inspection
- Third-party inspection if needed
- Acceptable defects
- Rework or replacement process
- Inspection timing before final payment

For a more detailed process, use the <a href="/blog/chinese-supplier-quality-control-checklist-before-bulk-order/">Chinese supplier quality control checklist</a>. Professional inspection support may be useful for higher-risk products or larger orders.

## Step 10: Confirm certification and compliance needs

Certification requirements depend on product and destination market.

Ask:

- What documents are available?
- Do certificates match the exact product?
- Does the product model appear on the certificate?
- Is the report date available?
- Does your destination market require anything else?
- Should you use professional compliance support?

Do not assume one document applies to every product, market, or sales channel.

## Step 11: Confirm defect handling and after-sales process

Discuss defect handling before you pay.

Confirm:

- What happens if defects are found before shipment?
- What happens if defects are found after delivery?
- Whether replacement, rework, credit, or discount is possible
- What proof is required
- Who handles correction costs
- What is confirmed in writing

The goal is not to predict every problem. The goal is to avoid having no process when a problem appears.

## Step 12: Keep written confirmation before payment

Keep a simple order file before paying.

Save:

- Quotation
- Proforma invoice
- Product specs
- Sample approval notes
- Packaging details
- Lead time
- Payment terms
- Trade terms
- Quality control plan
- Defect handling
- Supplier contact person

Do not rely on verbal promises only. Written confirmation helps both sides work from the same facts.

## First order confirmation message template

Copy and adapt this message before confirming your first order:

```text
Hello [Supplier Name],

Before we confirm the first order and arrange payment, please confirm the following details in writing:

1. Final product specification
2. Approved sample reference
3. Order quantity and MOQ
4. Unit price and what is included
5. Packaging and labeling details
6. Production lead time and when it starts
7. Payment terms
8. Trade terms and shipping responsibility
9. Quality control or inspection process
10. Certification or test documents if available
11. Defect handling process
12. Contact person for production updates

Please confirm these details before we arrange payment.

Thank you.
```

## Red flags before placing your first order

Be careful when:

- The supplier avoids written confirmation
- Product specs are vague
- Sample reference is unclear
- Price does not mention packaging
- Lead time changes often
- Payment terms are unclear
- The supplier pushes payment before details are confirmed
- Quality control process is vague
- Certification documents do not match the product
- Communication becomes inconsistent

These are not automatic deal-breakers, but they are reasons to slow down and clarify.

## Common mistakes small buyers make

### Paying before confirming specs

If specifications are vague before payment, they will be harder to correct after production starts.

### Assuming sample equals bulk production

Sample approval does not guarantee bulk production quality.

### Not confirming packaging

Packaging affects cost, appearance, damage risk, labeling, and buyer experience.

### Not understanding trade terms

Trade terms affect responsibility and cost. Ask for plain-language confirmation when you are unsure.

### Not planning inspection

Inspection should be discussed before production, not only after goods are finished.

### Not saving written records

Chats are easy to lose. Keep a clean order record with the key details.

### Comparing only price

The lowest price may hide missing packaging, unclear terms, weak quality control, or poor communication.
